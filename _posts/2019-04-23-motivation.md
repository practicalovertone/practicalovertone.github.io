## Motivation

As a music theory nerd (who didn't have a chance to study music theory formally in school), I'm always itching for more knowledge in this space. As a barbershopper, I'm always looking for interesting ways of applying new knowledge to our art form. While this blog will primarily have barbershop examples and tutorials, I hope that the concepts and ramblings can apply to any musical form.

I discovered the analysis of vocal signals while potentially looking into developing my own tool to perform the same task. For years, in personal vocal lessons, barbershop coaching sessions, and standing on the risers at rehearsals I've come across seemingly conflicting instructions for a specific vocal production goal. Many times, this instruction involves some sort of subjective language to describe a particular concept. Some of these terms:

* Bright
* Pressed
* Tight
* Dark
* Dopey

I've always had a problem connecting with these subjective terms and always thought I just didn't have the proper ear training to hear the subtle differences in these vocal timbres. My research into analysis of vocal signals was a hope that there could be an objective, third-party tool that we point to when conveying one of these subjective terms. This is where I found [VoceVista](https://www.vicevista.com).

VoceVista is precisely what I was thinking about developing and really shines at visualizing things that we barbershoppers really care about: overtones. We'll be going deeper into VoceVista in the future, but as a teaser, below is the kind of thing that VoceVista can represent for us. We're visualizing not just the notes being sung, but also the corresponding overtones from the fundamental harmonics produced by the singers.

![comparison](/assets/images/00-Motivation/Comparison.png)

We are looking at the same passage, of the same song, in the same key by two different quartets. The left quartet is **a Top 50 quartet**, while the right quartet is **a Top 10 quartet**. There is a clear visual difference between the two quartets [[^1]]. I'm immensely curious on what exactly causes these differences and if using this type of visual can help quartets or choruses improve their sound by visualizing some of the concepts that might have been harder to grasp for the average barbershopper in the past. 

## Hypothesis

##### Vocal analysis is to the voice as music theory was to written music

Music was written for hundreds of years before we formally started analyzing its structure. In the same way, I'm hoping vocal analysis will give us the same insight into the voice and making us overall better singers.
